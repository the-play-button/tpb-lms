# 🧭 How To Learn A Niche

**You'll end up focused on one niche. But at the very start you run a few in parallel** and
watch who bites hardest, then double down on the winner. So you need a fast, repeatable way to
get fluent in any niche. This is that method. The modules that follow apply it to each niche
we're testing.

Getting fluent in a niche means four things: **their words, their pain, their timing, and
their guard.** Build these four and you stop sounding like a vendor and start sounding like
someone from their world.

## 1. Their words: the lexicon

The job: in the first thirty seconds, they should feel you know their world. You get there by
speaking their vocabulary, not yours.

Build a glossary, grouped by:

- **Roles and structure** (who does what inside the company)
- **Metrics and KPIs** (the numbers they watch daily)
- **Operations** (how their work actually flows)
- **Tools and stack** (what they run)
- **Clients and suppliers** (who they answer to)

**Where you get it:** their job postings (roles and jargon are spelled out), their websites,
industry reports, trade associations, the LinkedIn profiles of people in the niche.

**Done looks like:** a term list you can drop naturally, so a real insider wouldn't guess
you're an outsider.

## 2. Their pain: the frustrations, by category

The job: name their problem before they do. That's what triggers "how do you know that?"

For **each pain**, capture five things:

- **The problem** in one line
- **The daily reality** (what it looks like on a Tuesday)
- **The trigger** that makes it worse
- **The impact** (what it costs them)
- **A real quote**, in their words

Cover the categories: **margin and revenue, talent and team, data and reporting, tech and
process, client management, growth, competition.**

**Where you get it:** Glassdoor and Indeed reviews, LinkedIn posts, industry forums, and above
all **your own calls** (you enrich this every week).

**Done looks like:** a table of pains, each with a quote. The quote is the weapon. You reuse
it almost word for word in discovery.

## 3. Their timing: buying triggers you can detect

This is the sharpest part. Don't guess who's hot. **Detect it.**

A trigger is an event that makes them start looking. For each one, write down:

- **The detectable signal** (repeated job postings, recent reviews, a LinkedIn post, a website
  change, a funding round, a news item)
- **The urgency** (now, 30 to 60 days, 60 to 90 days)
- **The pain level** (score it, so you rank who to call first)

**How you detect it:** LinkedIn Sales Navigator alerts, Google Alerts, headcount tracking,
Glassdoor review monitoring, website-change monitoring, funding databases.

Then keep **one discovery question per trigger** to confirm it live ("I saw you're hiring five
roles in two weeks, what's driving that?"), and score trigger strength (several signals plus a
deadline plus a decision-maker involved equals high urgency).

**Done looks like:** a list of `trigger → signal → how to detect → score`. That list is your
target list. You stop prospecting at random and start calling people who just signaled.

## 4. Their guard: the objections specific to this niche

The job: anticipate the objections you hear **only** in this niche, not the generic ones.

For each, note **why they say it** (the business context behind it) and **the real concern**
underneath. The answer itself depends on our current offer, so keep this section about
understanding their guard, not scripting a rebuttal.

**Done looks like:** you're never surprised by a niche-specific pushback.

## Why several niches at once

At the start we don't know which niche reacts best, so we run a handful in parallel, apply
this method to each, and read the responses. The niche that replies fastest, qualifies
cleanest, and closes hardest is the one we focus on. The modules that follow are the niches
we're testing. Each one is this method, filled in.
