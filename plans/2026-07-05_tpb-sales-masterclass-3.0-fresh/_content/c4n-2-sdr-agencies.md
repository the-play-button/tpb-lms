# 🏢 ICP: External SDR Agencies

**Who they are:** companies that provide outsourced SDR teams to other businesses (SDR-as-a-
service). They bill their clients per meeting or per pipeline generated, so rep productivity is
their profit. Typical shape: 10 to 100 reps, running HubSpot plus a dialer, US-based, scale-up
stage. The buyer is the **Head of SDRs, Sales Director, VP Sales, or founder.**

This is the niche we have the most depth on. Here it is through the four-part method.

## Their words

Speak these and you sound like one of them:

- **Structure:** client (the company hiring the agency) vs end client (the prospects), account
  manager, SDR manager, campaign manager, white-label, dedicated vs shared SDRs, **bench**
  (reps not yet assigned), **ramp time** (time to productive).
- **Metrics:** **seats** (billable units), utilization rate, **margin per seat**, meetings per
  SDR per month, cost per meeting, meeting show rate, client retention, client churn, SQL rate.
- **Operations:** sequence, cadence, touch, multi-threading, connect rate, positive response
  rate, dial sessions.
- **Commercials:** SOW, SLA, retainer, weekly and monthly business reviews (WBR/MBR).

## Their pain

The frustrations that keep the owner up at night, by category:

- **Margin compression:** clients push rates down while SDR salaries rise. *"We're stuck
  between client price pressure and rising SDR salaries."*
- **SDR turnover:** 40 to 60% a year. Constant recruiting, training, replacing. *"We spend more
  time hiring than managing performance."*
- **Inconsistent performance:** top rep does 3x the bottom rep. Hard to promise clients a
  number. *"Same training, same lists, wildly different results."*
- **Data quality:** 30 to 40% of contact data is wrong. Wasted dials, bounced emails, angry
  clients. *"We pay for 10,000 contacts but only 6,000 are reachable."*
- **Reporting hell:** clients want detailed reports, data is scattered across tools. *"I spend
  10 hours a week just making reports look good."*
- **Scope creep:** clients keep adding requirements without raising budget. Margins erode.

## Their timing: triggers you can detect

Each of these is spottable from the outside:

| Trigger | Signal to detect | Where |
|---|---|---|
| Rapid scaling | 5+ SDR job postings in 30 days | LinkedIn, job boards |
| Data-quality crisis | Postings for "CRM admin" / "data entry", posts about CRM cleanup | Job boards, LinkedIn |
| Client wants live reporting | Postings for "data analyst / reporting", "dashboards" on the site | Job boards, website |
| Onboarding-time crisis | Multiple entry-level SDR posts, "fast ramp" language | Job boards |
| Team burnout | Many SDR posts in 30 days, Glassdoor mentions of turnover | Glassdoor |
| Client audit failure | Postings for "compliance / QA", "audit-ready" on the site | Job boards, website |

Detect with LinkedIn Sales Navigator alerts, Glassdoor monitoring, job-board tracking, and
website-change monitoring. Rank by how many signals fire at once.

## Their guard: niche-specific objections

Things you hear **only** from agencies (understand the concern behind each):

- *"Our clients mandate their own tools."* → concern: contract compliance with clients.
- *"We can't change our process for every client."* → concern: operational complexity across
  many client setups.
- *"Our margins are already tight."* → concern: any spend has to clearly pay back.
- *"Our SDRs are already overwhelmed with tools."* → concern: adoption and tool fatigue.
- *"Clients want to see our manual work."* → concern: they equate effort with value.
- *"Client data security is paramount."* → concern: they're custodians of someone else's data.

## Why they were our first test

Agencies feel data pain hardest (high volume, dirty CRMs threaten their business model), they
have budget, they decide fast, and they're easy to reach. That made them the natural first
niche to test. Whether they stay the focus depends on who reacts best across all the niches we
run.
